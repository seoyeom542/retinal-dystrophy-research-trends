Background · a plain-language introduction

What is cone dystrophy?

Cone dystrophy is an inherited retinal disorder in which the retina's cone photoreceptors progressively lose function. It belongs to the larger category of inherited retinal dystrophies (IRDs) and is one of their most representative members.

Cones and rods

The human retina has two kinds of light-sensing photoreceptor cells.

  • Cone cells — work in bright light and are responsible for colour vision, central vision (reading, recognising faces) and visual acuity. They are concentrated in the central retina (the macula).
  • Rod cells — work in dim light and are responsible for black-and-white peripheral vision and night vision.

In cone dystrophy the cones are affected first and predominantly, so colour-vision disturbance and loss of central vision stand out. When rods degenerate alongside the cones, the condition is called cone-rod dystrophy.

Main symptoms

SymptomDescription
Reduced visionCentral vision gradually declines, making fine detail and text hard to see.
Colour-vision disturbanceThe ability to distinguish colours weakens (acquired colour blindness).
PhotophobiaUnusual discomfort in bright light.
Central scotomaA blurred or blind spot may appear in the centre of the visual field.

Interestingly, because rods are relatively preserved, people with cone dystrophy sometimes see better in dim conditions (day blindness).

Genetic causes

Cone dystrophy is genetically heterogeneous — many different genes can cause it. Genes that appear frequently in this project's literature analysis include:

  • ABCA4 — associated with Stargardt disease and cone-rod dystrophy
  • GUCA1A, GUCY2D — cone and cone-rod dystrophy
  • KCNV2 — cone dystrophy with a characteristic electroretinogram
  • PDE6C, PDE6H, CNGA3, CNGB3 — cone-function genes
  • RPGR — associated with the X-linked form

Inheritance can be autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, or X-linked, depending on the causative gene.

Diagnosis

  • Electroretinography (ERG) — measures the electrical responses of cones and rods to tell which cells are affected
  • Optical coherence tomography (OCT) — images cross-sections of the retina to reveal structural change
  • Fundus autofluorescence (FAF) — assesses the retinal pigment epithelium
  • Genetic testing — confirms the causative variant, securing the diagnosis and guiding treatment eligibility

Treatment and outlook

There is currently no cure for cone dystrophy, but research is advancing fast. As this site's dashboard shows, gene therapy leads the field, alongside gene editing (CRISPR), stem cells, and optogenetics. A gene therapy (Luxturna) is already approved for retinal disease caused by RPE65 variants — heralding an era in which a precise genetic diagnosis can lead directly to treatment. You can browse the active clinical trials on this site.
